<p>
This week I wrote the my first ruby method that I would actually use. I used to struggle with it day in and day out: As an 8th grade social studies teacher with Chicago Public Schools, the need to endlessly be coming up with different permutations and groups of students. My method puts them into nice random groups of 3 or 4, but I'm confident at this point that I could change the number or add other factors if I needed to and had the time to think it out.
</p>
<p>
So that was pretty great. It isn't a complete program by any means, but to see myself making something that actually would have a use, that would have saved me time and headaches in the classroom. I can remember <em>googling around,</em> looking for exactly this type of program, and it's cool to see.
</p>
<p>
It also reminds me of some of the things I <strong>most hate</strong> about tech, or at least my perception of it: This idea that the best minds in the tech community are going to right failing school systems through some application of the proper code and algorithms to students- to my students. Or that my kids would learn better if we could just get a grant to put an iPad in each of their hands because they are <em>digital natives</em>, and anyhow the iPad has this app that has been shown to boost a student's reading score...
</p>
<p style="padding: .5em "> So, I'm pretty emotional about this, but I'll try to be rational from here.
</p>
<p>
My first direct experience with standardized testing as a new teacher was sitting with the rest of my team on a lunch break looking over our kids' scores (yes, we work on breaks and any other time there is a child in sight). This wasn't a typical test with one score per subject, or even two or three: Every single skill (at least as defined by the <a href="http://www.corestandards.org/">Common Core State Standards Initiative</a>) was listed with its own score out of 100. Finding the main idea. Identifying the purpose of a text. Interrogating for bias or point of view. In my opinion, good skills to have!
</p>
<p>
Many of my students had scores in the 20s though. Did they only get 20% of the questions right? No- The scores were rankings. Each of my 13-year-old children had emerged from their testing with a number showing their percentile across all students who'd taken the test that year. Now I was spending my lunch break sorting my kids into groups so I could later split them up and hammer <em>find main ideas</em> into some little heads while my colleague across the hall pounded <em>cite evidence to support an argument</em> into others.
</p>
<p>
It sounds so simple, even beautiful from the outside. It sounds like a solution I would have tried to google if it wasn't already there. I struggled day in and day out to find ways to get the best possible curriculum for each of my kids to each of them and it drove me up the wall at times.
</p>
<p>
The fact is that I did not learn to read or write by doing main idea worksheets and I never had a teacher who saw me, ranked, and used that to target my deficiencies. Maybe an app would make the whole process less cold, more interesting or colorful or just friendly. Maybe it would vibrate. I don't care because none of this is what education is about for me.
</p>
<p>
I'll also say that I grew up in a middle-to-upper class neighborhood with a fully-stocked fridge, an incredible arts program in every school, sports in the evenings (mostly soccer, but I also played a difficult season each of little leage baseball and basketball) and parents who had the time to help me every step of the way. Both worked, but only one job each, with benefits, and never at night. I read with one of them almost every night. My father coached soccer and was a scout leader. I can remember him teaching me geometry (how to find the area of a triangle) on a folded paper napkin. I don't feel guilty for having any of these things and don't mean to suggest that others don't, but discussions around school achievement that would pretend such factors are immaterial are in my opinion irrelevant discussions. Attempts to rank students without controlling for myriad social and economic factors outside of a school are simply doomed.
</p>
<p>
I've always been a person oriented towards finding solutions. It's a reason I'm enjoying learning to code so far. And yet I understand that there is a human element that I won't ever replicate- to the point that sometimes even trying debases us. I want to write code to make the world better, to do so conscientiously and with compassion but also without the delusion that an app can fix an oppressive social relationship on its own. It's something that I have struggled with and will continue to wrestle, probably for the rest of my life.
</p>
