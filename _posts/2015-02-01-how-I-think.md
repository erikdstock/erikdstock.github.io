<p style="margin-left:7%"><em>I've been asked to write about the way I think with special reference to the <a href="http://www.thelearningweb.net/personalthink.html">Gregorc Mind Style</a>. OK!</em></p>
<p>
After going through several years of teacher school and more childhood psych theorists than I can count, I've learned to take a lot of the pop schema that come and go in discussions of psychology with a grain of salt. Some ideas, like that of "right-brained" and "left-brained" people, are essentially discredited at this point. Others simply clash with one another or seem designed to sell self-help programs. Even so, I believe all of these theories have something to offer by giving us a framework to examine the way our minds work. Even Freud.
</p>
<p>
I guess that is sort of where I am looking at my Gregorc mind style. I scored pretty much evenly across the board -which might just show I'm afraid to express my opinion on a web form- but reading further into <a href="http://web.cortland.edu/andersmd/learning/gregorc.htm">Gregorc's Model</a>, it seemed like a useful way of thinking about the way my mind works.
</p>
<p>
So at the with all the caveats of self-diagnosis I'm going to say that I <em>identify</em> most with the <strong>Abstract Sequential</strong> and <strong>Concrete Random</strong> styles of thinking. A few illustrations of this just in my work so far with Dev Bootcamp:
</p>
<ul>
<li>I love the problem-solving aspect of DBC. I sometimes get hung up on problems even after I've solved them, knowing that I have another half-baked idea and want to see if I can make that work too.</li>
<li>I'm prone to falling down rabbit holes. I was on track to finish this week's work a full day early. That didn't happen because I decided to change the styling on my nav bar above. I'd like to call this "experimenting to find answers."</li>
<li>I definitely don't do super well with lots of specific rules and regulations. It's one of the main headaches for me with CSS styling right now: I want to understand how the pieces fit together every time.</li>
<li>Likewise, I've never been the best at keeping very detailed records. I'm glad this blog is required for that exact reason.</li>
</ul>
<p>
I'm looking at my weaknesses  according to my self-diagnosed thinking styles, now. This is difficult, because I feel like really <strong>most</strong> of the weaknesses apply to me across all categories. The ones that stick out the most, the ones that line up with my biggest problems in DBC are easy to pick out: Having too little time to deal with a subject thoroughly, definitely. One of the most difficult things I ever have to do is simply letting something go with the faith I will pick it up in more detail later. I really, really want to know how and why it works right now.
</p>
<p>
One final note. I don't mind working with other people, and I don't think of myself as always competetive. I just don't agree with those particular things. I generally consider myself (and internet quizzes confirm) a pretty flexible, multi-modal learner. My biggest challenges are around time management, distractions and keeping up motivation when stimulating problems give way to feelings of impossibility or drudgery. I've been working to address these challenges for years with mixed results. So far, I feel like DBC is helping with this.
</p>
